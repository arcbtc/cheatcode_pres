Slide 2
Title : Breif history of free & open source
* 1970s — “Sharing culture”  
  Homebrew Computer Club (pre–open source)
* 1976 — Bill Gates, “An Open Letter to Hobbyists”  
  “People copying software is stealing”
* 1983 — GNU Project (Richard Stallman)  
  1985 — GNU GPL v1  
  * Must share source  
  * Keeps software open  
  * Requires attribution  
* 1987 — MIT License (Massachusetts Institute of Technology)  
  * Do anything (even use in closed software)  
  * Just keep attribution  

Notes: "Computer enthusiats early on had a culture of sharing, if you needed a piece of softwre to do a thing you would ask about if your freinds had similiar software or build your own. first few generations of desktop computers would start in the command line - the commodor 64 "READY" prompt.
When propriatory forces like microsoft came along, they wanted to ip and own software. 
A reaction to treating software as property rather than knowledge, Stallman quit MIT and began the GNU project 'GNU Not Unix'.
Stallmans first challenge was securing 'free' software with a license, that stopped propriatory interests owning the software later, and kept attribute as to where the software came from.
Later other licenses like MIT allowed software to be used privately, but attribution should be given by including the original license.
In the mid 1990s companies like Microsoft started to become twitchy when Linus Torvalds, in a modest bungalow in san fransisco, and Swanseas very own Alan Cox, in a small fisherman cottage, were running and mainting a stable linux version. <insert anicdotal story about visiting alan cox and him swearing he had never touched bitcoin even though his main workstation was an old bitcoin miner>.
One of Linus's innovations for managing the software later became GIT, which is how all free and open souce software is now managed."

Slide 3
Title: Free & Open Source Won (Quietly)
* The internet runs on open source  
  (Linux, Apache, PostgreSQL, OpenSSL)
* Every company builds on FOSS  
  Even proprietary software depends on it
* Big tech became the biggest contributors  
  Microsoft, Google, Meta all fund & maintain FOSS
* Open source became infrastructure  
  Invisible, but everywhere
"By the 2000s, something interesting happened.
Open source didn’t just survive — it won.
The entire internet stack is basically open source.
Linux, web servers, databases, cryptography…
Even companies building closed software are standing on top of it.
And then something even more ironic happened —
The biggest open source contributors… became big tech.
Microsoft, Google, Meta — all heavily funding and maintaining open source.
So you end up in this weird world where:
The foundations are permissionless…
but the products built on top are often permissioned.
"
